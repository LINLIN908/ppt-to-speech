# Q&A Preparation Methodology

The Q&A section is what distinguishes this skill from "write me speaker notes". A presenter who has rehearsed answers to the right eight questions walks in calm. The right questions are found by attacking the deck, not by summarizing it.

**Theoretical grounding.** The three-pass method operationalizes Toulmin's full six-element argument model. The six elements are: claim (the conclusion being asserted), data (the evidence offered), warrant (the inferential link from data to claim), backing (the basis that makes the warrant itself valid), qualifier (the word limiting how strongly the claim is asserted — "suggests," "in most cases"), and rebuttal (the conditions under which the claim doesn't hold). Questions distribute across these elements by archetype: novice questions probe missing *data* and undefined terms; expert questions attack *warrants* ("why does that evidence support that conclusion?") and *backing* ("and what makes that reasoning itself valid?") — backing is where the deepest methodological challenges live; the skeptic pass hunts for the weakest warrant in the deck's central claim, then asks whether the backing for that warrant actually holds. Finding questions means finding the argument's joints.

## How to find the questions

Work through the extracted deck with three passes, one per archetype:

### Pass 1 — Novice

Simulate someone smart but outside the field. Scan for:
- Jargon used without definition (acronyms, model names, method names). The question is "What is X, in plain terms?"
- Steps that are obvious to the author but skipped on the slides ("wait, where did the data come from?")
- The motivation gap: "Why does this matter to someone like me?"

Novice questions are dangerous precisely because they're easy — a fumbled plain-language answer damages credibility more than a fumbled technical one. Model answers must contain zero jargon and at most one analogy.

### Pass 2 — Expert

Simulate the most informed person who could be in the room (committee member in the subfield; a VC who has funded a competitor). Scan for:
- Every methodological choice that had a plausible alternative → "Why X and not Y?"
- Every number → "How was this measured / what's the denominator / over what window?"
- Every comparison or claim of novelty → "How does this differ from [closest specific prior work / competitor]?" Name the prior work or competitor in the question; vague expert questions are unrealistic.
- Boundary conditions → "Does this hold when [assumption] breaks?"

### Pass 3 — Skeptic

Find the single weakest load-bearing claim in the whole deck — the inferential leap, the suspiciously clean number, the unaddressed elephant. Write the question the presenter hopes nobody asks. There is exactly one or two of these; don't manufacture hostility everywhere.

## Format for each prepared question

```
**Q[n] · [Novice/Expert/Skeptic] — likely from [who]**
Question: ...
Why they ask: one line — what gap or doubt triggers it.
Model answer: 2–4 spoken sentences, same language and register as the script.
If stuck: one fallback line (scenario-appropriate "I don't know" — see scenario reference).
```

## Quality bar for model answers

- Spoken register: contractions fine, no citations-with-years read aloud, no bullet lists — a person has to say this.
- Answer the question in the first sentence, then support. Presenters under pressure deliver the first sentence and improvise the rest; front-load the value.
- Concede honestly where the deck is genuinely weak. A model answer that bluffs teaches the presenter to bluff, and experts smell it.
- Keep answers shorter than the presenter thinks they should be. 30 seconds spoken (~70 English words / ~100 字) is the ceiling for most answers; long answers invite follow-ups into weak territory.
