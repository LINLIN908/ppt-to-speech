# Panel / Roundtable Scenario

For conference panels, roundtables, and symposium discussions. This scenario breaks the deck-centric mold: there may be no slides at all, or just one or two. The deliverable shifts from a per-slide script to a *position kit* — an opening statement, defensible talking points, and reaction material. The audience judges panelists on clarity of viewpoint and quality of interaction, not completeness.

**Theoretical grounding.** Panels are exercises in ethos and stance-taking (Aristotle; Hyland's metadiscourse work on writer/speaker positioning): the speaker must commit to a position visible enough to disagree with. Cognitive load theory applies doubly — the audience is tracking four speakers at once, so each contribution must be a single self-contained point with its own beginning and end.

## What to produce (instead of a per-slide script)

1. **Opening statement (2–5 min, confirm the allotted time):** the panelist's position on the panel theme, structured as: stance in one sentence → the strongest piece of evidence or experience behind it → one implication that invites discussion. If the user has a deck, treat slides as backdrop for this statement, not as a sequence to narrate.
2. **Three to five talking points:** each one paragraph of spoken prose, self-contained, deployable in any order. Each point gets a one-line "bridge-in" so the panelist can attach it to whatever the moderator or co-panelists just said ("That connects to something we see in classrooms — ...").
3. **Anticipated moderator questions:** derive from the panel title/abstract if the user provides it; otherwise generate from the panel's likely theme and ask the user to confirm. Moderators ask synthesis questions ("where is the field going"), contrast questions ("you two seem to disagree — do you?"), and audience-proxy questions.
4. **Reaction material:** the panel-specific addition. If the user names co-panelists or their positions, prepare one respectful-disagreement line and one build-on line per co-panelist. Disagreement formula: validate the frame, then pivot on evidence — "I agree the cost barrier is real; where I'd push back is whether cost is the *binding* constraint — in our data it was teacher confidence."

## Tone

- Conversational authority: contractions, first person, concrete anecdotes over citations. A panel is the one academic setting where reading from a script aloud is a visible failure — so write the material to be *internalized*, and say so to the user: these are paragraphs to rehearse until they compress into recall cues, not pages to read.
- Commit to a stance. "It depends" with no follow-through is the panel equivalent of narrating your outline. Every talking point should be specific enough that a reasonable colleague could disagree.
- Brevity is status. The strongest panelist speaks in 45–90 second units and stops cleanly. Mark each talking point with its spoken duration.

## Q&A specifics for panels

- Audience Q&A is shared across panelists; prepare for the *jump-ball* dynamic — the script should mark which 2–3 anticipated questions this panelist should actively claim ("I can take that one") because they sit in their strength zone, and which to deflect gracefully to co-panelists.
- Novice questions dominate panel Q&A (panels draw broader audiences than paper sessions): prepare plain-language versions of every technical term in the talking points.
- Skeptic question: usually challenges the panel's premise itself ("isn't this whole agenda hype?"). Prepare one answer that defends the premise without defensiveness — concede the kernel of truth, then anchor on the most concrete evidence available.
- The graceful "I don't know" on a panel: hand it off as generosity, not retreat — "Honestly outside what I've studied — but I suspect [co-panelist] has seen this directly."

## Language notes

- Same bilingual conventions as other academic scenarios; in Chinese panel settings (圆桌论坛), openings are minimal —「谢谢主持人」 then straight to the stance. Flowery throat-clearing reads as junior.
