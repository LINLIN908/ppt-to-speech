# Conference Talk Scenario

For paper presentations at academic conferences (10–15 minute slots, strict moderation). The audience is peers whose attention is scarce — they've sat through six talks already and are deciding within 90 seconds whether to listen or check email. The goal is different from a defense: not to prove worthiness, but to be remembered, cited, and approached afterward.

**Theoretical grounding.** Apply Mayer's redundancy principle aggressively — conference decks following Alley's assertion–evidence style (one full-sentence claim + visual evidence per slide) leave the spoken narration to carry the argument, so the script must add reasoning, not echo text. Use a light version of Monroe's attention step: even academic audiences need a hook before they grant you their working memory (Sweller — sustained listening is costly; earn it early).

## Tone

- Warmer and more energetic than a defense, still precise. You are sharing a finding you're excited about with colleagues, not being examined.
- One claim, said confidently, beats three claims hedged. The paper carries the full nuance; the talk carries the one idea.
- It is acceptable — encouraged — to say "the details are in the paper" and move on. A defense can never do this; a conference talk must.

## Structural conventions

The cardinal constraint is the time box. A 12-minute slot fits roughly one idea, one method sketch, two results, one takeaway. The script's main job is *triage*:

- **Opening (20–30s):** Hook first, then name/affiliation/title. Good academic hooks: the puzzle ("Every CT intervention we reviewed measures learning; almost none measures transfer"), the surprising number from your own results, or the gap stated as a provocation. Do not open with an outline slide narration — cut "First I will talk about..., then..." entirely; the structure should be felt, not announced.
- **Related work:** 30 seconds maximum, framed as "what's missing", never as a literature tour. Name only the one or two works your contribution directly builds on or contradicts.
- **Method:** A sketch sufficient to make results believable. Move all robustness detail to a backup slide and have the script note which anticipated question each backup slide answers.
- **Results:** The heart — spend 40% of total time here. Walk one figure slowly rather than four figures fast. The script should verbalize how to read the key figure ("each dot is a classroom; the gap between the lines is the effect").
- **Takeaway slide:** One sentence the audience can repeat at dinner. Write this sentence first; it is the talk's thesis and everything else exists to earn it.
- **Closing:** Takeaway + "I'd love to talk more — find me at the poster session / here's my contact" + invite questions. Conference closings are invitations, not conclusions.

## Q&A specifics for conference talks

- Sessions allow 2–3 questions; answers must be 30–45 seconds. The prep should emphasize *compression*: first sentence answers, second sentence supports, then offer to continue offline.
- The "take it offline" move is legitimate and professional here (unlike a defense): "That's exactly the analysis we're doing next — I'd love to compare notes after the session."
- Expert questions to prepare: generalizability beyond your sample; the closest competing explanation for the result; "did you try X" for the one obvious alternative method.
- Novice/outsider questions: conferences mix subfields — prepare a 15-second plain-language version of the core construct and the practical "so what" for someone in an adjacent area.
- Skeptic question: usually targets external validity or a suspiciously large effect size. The model answer concedes scope honestly and points to what the paper *does* nail down.
- Also prepare for the non-question question (a commenter promoting their own work): the script's model response is one gracious sentence acknowledging the connection, then redirect: "Thanks — that connects to our limitation on X; happy to discuss after."

## Language notes

- International conferences run in English; if the presenter is a non-native speaker, favor even shorter sentences and flag hard-to-pronounce terms in delivery cues with a simpler synonym in parentheses.
- Chinese domestic conferences (学术年会) follow the same triage logic; openings are brief —「各位老师、同行，大家好」— and the offline invitation is equally standard (「欢迎会后交流」).
