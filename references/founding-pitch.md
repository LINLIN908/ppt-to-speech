# Founding Pitch Scenario

For investor pitches, demo days, and startup competitions. The audience decides in minutes whether to keep listening; the script's job is momentum.

**Theoretical grounding.** The spoken arc maps onto Monroe's Motivated Sequence (attention → need → satisfaction → visualization → action). Evidence from entrepreneurship research shapes the priorities: investors reward demonstrated preparedness over displayed passion (Chen, Yao & Kotha, 2009, AMJ) — which is why the Q&A prep on unit economics matters as much as the script itself — and first impressions form within the opening minute (Clark, 2008), which is why the hook precedes the introduction.

## Tone

- Confident declaratives. "We reduce onboarding time by 60%" — not "we believe we may be able to help reduce...". Investors discount everything by default; hedged claims round to zero.
- Concrete over abstract. Every claim gets a number, a name, or a moment: a customer quote, a revenue figure, a date.
- Plain words. If a slide says "leveraging synergistic AI capabilities", the script should say what the product actually does in words a smart 15-year-old follows.

## Structural conventions

Pitch decks vary, but the spoken arc should always be: hook → problem → solution → proof → ask.

- **Opening (15–20s):** Never "Hi, today I'll present...". Open with the hook: a startling number, a one-sentence customer story, or the contrarian belief the company is built on. Name and company come *after* the hook lands.
- **Problem slides:** Make it personal and expensive. The script should answer "who bleeds, and how much money does the bleeding cost?" before the solution appears.
- **Solution/demo slides:** Shortest sentences in the whole script. One capability per breath. If there's a demo, the script must include the recovery line for when the demo fails ("While that loads — the result you'll see is...").
- **Traction slide:** Slow down. This is the defense's "method slide" equivalent — investors form their questions here. Speak the numbers and their time window precisely: "twelve thousand MAU, up 40% month-over-month since March."
- **Team slide:** One sentence per person, formula: unfair advantage, not biography. "Lin built the robotics curriculum used in 200 schools" beats a degree list.
- **The ask:** Say the number out loud and what it buys: "We're raising $1.5M to get to 100 paying schools in 18 months." Many founders mumble the ask; the script should mark it `[slow down — the ask]`.
- **Closing:** End on the vision in one sentence, then stop. No "thank you slide" narration, no trailing off.

## Q&A specifics for pitches

- The graceful "I don't know": never say "I don't know" flat in a pitch. The pattern is: answer the adjacent question you *can* answer, then commit to follow-up: "We haven't run that cohort analysis yet — what we do know is retention at 30 days is 62% — and I can send the full breakdown this week."
- Expert (investor) questions to always prepare: unit economics (CAC, LTV, margin); why now; what happens when an incumbent copies this; the bottom-up version of the market-size math; current runway and burn.
- Novice questions (a generalist partner, press, demo-day audience): what the product literally is; who pays and how much; "how is this different from ChatGPT/[obvious incumbent]?" — founders are bad at this one because the answer feels too obvious to them.
- Skeptic question: attack the weakest link in the revenue logic (usually willingness-to-pay or distribution, not technology). The model answer should concede the risk exists and show the cheapest experiment that retires it.

## Language notes

- Chinese-language pitches (路演) tolerate slightly more formality but the hook-first rule still applies. Avoid translated Silicon Valley jargon (「赋能」「抓手」「闭环」) in the spoken script; use plain verbs.
- Keep brand names, metrics (MAU, ARR, CAC), and product terms in English even in Chinese delivery — that is how the room talks.
